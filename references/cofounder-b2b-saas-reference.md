# FreelanceOS — Co-founder Reference

**Role:** Co-founder
**Tenure:** June 2017 – January 2019
**Location:** Waterloo, ON

---

## What It Was

FreelanceOS was a B2B SaaS platform for independent freelancers — primarily designers, developers, and writers — to manage the operational layer of their business: contracts, invoicing, payment reminders, and client communication. We built it during my final year at Waterloo (Systems Design Engineering) and ran it for 18 months after graduating.

The insight: freelancers were stitching together Google Docs for contracts, PayPal for payments, Gmail for follow-ups, and nothing for tracking who owed them what. The friction wasn't just annoying — it was costing them repeat business. If a freelancer had to chase an invoice three times, they were less likely to work with that client again. We thought: if you make the administrative layer invisible, you free up the creative layer.

We built it in a summer, launched in the fall, and had 800+ paying customers at $180K ARR when we wound down.

---

## How We Got to $180K ARR

**The product:** Contract templates with e-signature, automated invoice generation, payment reminders (email + SMS), a client-facing portal for approving deliverables and paying invoices, and a dashboard showing outstanding payments, upcoming deadlines, and client health. We launched with the core payments and contracts flow and added the client portal in month 6.

**The zero-to-one moment:** We launched a waiting list from a landing page 3 months before we had a product. Got 400 signups from a Product Hunt launch, another 200 from a Reddit post in r/freelance. When we opened the beta, we had 80 paying customers on day one. That told us we had the right problem before we had the solution.

**Growth without paid acquisition:** We grew to 800+ paying freelancers through two channels:
- Content SEO: articles on "how to write a freelance contract," "freelance invoice template," "how to charge a late payment fee" — long-tail, high-intent. These compounded over 18 months and eventually drove the majority of organic signups.
- Referral loops built into the product: when a freelancer sent a contract or invoice to a client, the client saw the FreelanceOS branding on the portal. We added "Created with FreelanceOS — start your free trial" to every client-facing page. That drove a meaningful percentage of new signups from clients who were also freelancers.

**Pricing:** $19/month flat. No tiers. We tried adding a premium tier at $49/month and it confused the signup flow more than it increased revenue. Kept it simple.

---

## Why We Wound Down

This was not a failure. We made a deliberate decision to close in January 2019 when I received the Intercom offer.

The path forward for FreelanceOS was to move upmarket — from individual freelancers to agencies and studios with 5-20 freelancers, where the coordination and billing problem was even more painful and the willingness to pay was higher. But that pivot would have required:
- A new product surface for team-level billing and contract management
- A sales motion (agencies don't sign up on a landing page)
- Significantly more capital than we had

We were a 3-person team (two engineers, one of whom was also co-founder) with $180K ARR and no external funding. The math on raising a seed round to go after the agency market didn't work for me — the market wasn't growing fast enough to attract the kind of capital that would make it worth the dilution.

I gave customers 90 days notice, helped the ones who wanted it migrate their data to alternatives (we wrote migration guides for FreshBooks and HoneyBook), and closed cleanly. No drama, no debt.

---

## What I Took From It

**Build it like you'll use it.** I was still doing freelance design work when we built FreelanceOS. I used the product to invoice my own clients. That meant I knew when the invoice reminder timing was wrong, when the contract template was missing a clause freelancers actually cared about, when the client portal was confusing. You can't fake that level of specificity. It shows up in the product.

**Talk to churned users.** Our most important product decisions came from talking to people who tried FreelanceOS and left. They told us exactly what was missing. The client portal came directly from a churned user who said "I still have to email the invoice separately — why doesn't it just go directly to my client?" We hadn't thought about the client-side experience at all until that conversation.

**Know when to stop.** This is underrated. A lot of founders hold on too long because stopping feels like failure. It isn't. Closing FreelanceOS cleanly was the right call for me, for my co-founders, and for our customers. The Intercom opportunity was better than the FreelanceOS-to-agency-market path. I made the call and I'd make it again.

---

## How to Frame in Interviews

**For 0-to-1 roles:** Lead with the build timeline (zero to paying customers in 11 weeks) and the product philosophy (build it like you'll use it). This demonstrates authentic product intuition, not just product methodology.

**For growth roles:** The SEO and referral flywheel story is the right frame. Two channels, no paid acquisition, compound growth over 18 months. It shows growth thinking without growth title experience.

**For founder-friendly cultures:** Don't hide the wind-down. Explain it as a deliberate decision based on clear analysis of the path forward. That's more impressive than implying it failed — it shows judgment under uncertainty.

**What to avoid:** Don't call it a "startup" without context — the scale is meaningful ($180K ARR, 800+ customers) but it's also clearly pre-seed/bootstrapped. Be matter-of-fact about the size. The story is good. Over-inflating it makes it worse.
