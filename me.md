## Work History — Key Stories

### Notion (Jan 2022 – Apr 2025): Senior PM, Enterprise

**Strategic context:**
Notion's growth story is bottoms-up — individuals and small teams adopt it for free, and eventually someone in IT notices and wants to bring it under governance. That transition from unmanaged team adoption to managed enterprise deployment is genuinely hard: the admin needs controls, the users don't want to lose their workflows, and procurement needs to justify a 7-figure contract. I owned that transition.

The core tension I worked on: Notion was designed for individuals first. The settings model, the permissions model, the content hierarchy — all of it was optimized for small teams. Scaling that to organizations with 500+ users and real compliance requirements meant rebuilding a lot of the primitives without breaking the thing that made Notion good.

**Workspace governance redesign:**
The problem: enterprise customers were churning or stuck in free tiers because their IT admins couldn't get control of the environment. You'd have 200 people using Notion in a company, and the IT admin had no visibility into who owned what, what was being shared externally, or how to roll out new features consistently. We rebuilt the workspace settings model — permission inheritance, admin audit logs, external sharing controls, member lifecycle management. Reduced time-to-activation by 40% among enterprise accounts, and NPS went up 12 points in the enterprise segment.

**Admin AI rollout framework:**
When Notion started rolling out AI features, the default was to turn them on for everyone. That's fine for a startup team; it's a compliance problem for a Fortune 500 company. I built the framework that let IT admins gate AI access by team, by use case, and by data type. This wasn't just a settings panel — it required a new permissions model, new audit trails, and a way to communicate AI usage back to admins in a way they could explain to their security team. 60% of enterprise accounts had adopted the framework within 3 months.

**PLG-to-enterprise conversion motion:**
The classic Notion pattern: a team uses Notion for free for 18 months, and then someone in procurement gets involved and nothing moves for 6 months. I built the upgrade path — the signals that told us a self-serve org was ready for enterprise, the in-product touchpoints that moved the conversation to procurement, the trial experience that let IT admins evaluate the enterprise tier before committing. Converted 15% of self-serve organizations into enterprise contracts in the first year of this motion.

**Workspace unification:**
This one was the most complex technically. Many large Notion customers had ended up with multiple workspaces — one for each department, sometimes dozens — because no one had built a clean migration path. Users had different logins, different content structures, different billing relationships. Workspace unification let enterprise customers collapse those into a single governed environment. It was the most-requested enterprise feature at the time of launch.

**Key numbers:** 40% reduction in time-to-activation, +12 enterprise NPS, 60% AI framework adoption, 15% PLG-to-enterprise conversion rate.

**Interview framing:** Lead with the governance redesign story because it shows diagnostic skill (finding the real activation blocker), system thinking (permissions model redesign), and enterprise empathy (understanding what IT admins actually need). The AI framework story is the most forward-looking and relevant to AI-first roles.

### Intercom (Mar 2019 – Dec 2021): PM II, SMB Growth

**Strategic context:**
Intercom in 2019 was navigating a classic SMB SaaS growth problem: lots of signups, low conversion. The company had a 14-day trial, and most small businesses were churning out before paying. The standard response was to optimize the onboarding flow — better tooltips, shorter time-to-first-value, that kind of thing. I inherited a roadmap full of that work.

What I found instead: the conversion problem wasn't about the end user at all. It was about the account owner — the person who actually owned the subscription and was responsible for upgrading. That person almost never saw the trial experience because the product was designed around the end users (support agents). By the time the trial expired, the account owner had no idea what they were paying for or whether it was working.

**The insight and the discovery process:**
I ran 3 experiments from the inherited roadmap before rejecting it. None of them moved the conversion number meaningfully. That gave me permission to go back upstream and ask what was actually causing 82% of trial drop-off. The answer was that drop-off was concentrated at the moment where an account owner action was required — account setup, billing, team invite — and account owners weren't in the product. They'd signed up their team but never came back.

**The build:**
Admin trial management hub. One surface where the account owner could see: who's using the product, what features they're using, whether the trial OKR (for their use case) is being met, and one-click upgrade. Collapsed 5 steps across 4 different surfaces into a single decision. Took 6 weeks to build the first version.

**Validation:**
Piloted with a 2-week prototype across 40K accounts. Moved conversion from 18% to 28% in that cohort. Took those results back to leadership and got approval to fund the full redesign. Full rollout moved conversion from 18% to 34% — 280K+ new seats, $18M+ in ARR.

**Key numbers:** 18% to 34% conversion, 280K+ seats, $18M+ ARR, pilot 18% to 28% lift, 40K account pilot.

**Interview framing:** This story is most powerful when told in causal order: inherited roadmap → ran experiments → rejected roadmap → went upstream → found the account owner as the missing persona → built around that insight → validated with a prototype → funded the full thing. Don't skip the middle. The insight is only credible if you show the work that got you there.

### FreelanceOS (Jun 2017 – Jan 2019): Co-founder

**What it was:**
B2B SaaS platform for freelancers to manage contracts, invoicing, and client communication. Built it with two friends during my last year at Waterloo and the year after graduating. The insight was that freelancers were stitching together Google Docs, PayPal, and email to manage their clients — and the friction was killing repeat business. If you made the admin layer invisible, freelancers would spend more time on actual work and less time chasing invoices.

**How we got to $180K ARR:**
Launched a waiting list while still in school, got 400 signups from a Product Hunt post, and had 80 paying customers on day one. Grew to 800+ paying freelancers over 18 months through content SEO — "freelance contract template", "how to invoice a client", that kind of thing — and referral loops built into the product. No paid acquisition.

**Why we wound down:**
Not a failure. We hit a ceiling because we were a 3-person team and the enterprise path (moving to agencies and larger firms) would have required a much bigger team and a much bigger capital base. I got the Intercom offer and it was clearly the better move. We shut down cleanly, gave customers 90 days notice, and helped them migrate.

**What I took from it:**
Doing the thing yourself changes what you build. I understood invoicing better than any PM I've met because I used the product to invoice my own freelance clients while building it. That sensibility — build it like you'll use it — has stayed with me.

**Interview framing:** Don't lead with the ARR if the role isn't about revenue. Lead with: I built a product from zero, got to paying customers in 11 weeks, and learned what it means to be responsible for the whole thing — not just the product, but the business.

## How I Work

- I write first. Before any meeting, any design review, any roadmap discussion, I write down what I think. It clarifies my thinking and makes the conversation more useful.
- I push on the "why now." If a feature has been on the roadmap for 6 months without shipping, I want to know why. Usually there's a real reason. Sometimes there isn't. Either way, naming it is better than pretending.
- I'm blunt in private, diplomatic in public. I'll tell you what I think in a 1:1. I won't undermine you in a meeting. I expect the same.
- I give engineers room to disagree with the solution, not the problem. The best engineers I've worked with have pushed back on my solutions. I've almost always been glad they did.
- I close the loop. If I asked you to do something, I track it. If I said I'd get back to you, I will. I find it deeply unprofessional when people don't do this — it signals they think their time is more valuable than yours.

## Key Terminology

- **PLG-to-enterprise motion:** Converting bottoms-up product adoption into top-down enterprise procurement
- **Workspace governance:** Admin controls, permissions, audit logs, and lifecycle management for large multi-user environments
- **Account owner persona:** In SMB SaaS, the person who owns the subscription is often not the person who uses the product daily — understanding this gap is a key conversion lever
- **Admin trial management:** Giving the account owner a dedicated surface to evaluate, manage, and upgrade a trial — rather than relying on end-user engagement signals
- **AI feature gating:** Admin-controlled rollout of AI capabilities — by team, use case, or data type — for enterprise compliance requirements
- **Time-to-activation:** From contract signed to first enterprise account fully configured and in active use
