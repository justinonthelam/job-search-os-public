# Target Companies List

Last updated: March 2026

---

## How This List Works

Two separate dimensions:

- **Brand Grade** — How a PM hiring manager reads this company on your resume. Prestige, talent bar reputation, product culture signal, name recognition in tech. Independent of personal preferences. Reflects the company's brand *today* — not its trajectory.
- **Priority Tier** — How this fits your actual job search priorities (AI-first products + PM scope + prestige + comp). Incorporates brand, product culture fit, AI depth, *and* growth trajectory. A fast-growing startup with a weak current brand can tier higher than a stagnant company with a stronger one — the tier reflects total opportunity, not just present-day signal.

---

## Brand Grade Scale

| Grade | What it means |
|-------|---------------|
| **S** | Elite. Universally signals top PM talent bar. FAANG-tier names, frontier AI labs. |
| **A** | Strong. Well-respected PM brand. Opens doors at most companies. |
| **B** | Solid. Recognized in tech circles. Good signal, not universally prestigious. |
| **C** | Known brand but not a meaningful PM prestige signal. |
| **D** | Niche or weak. Recognizable in a specific industry or circle, limited signal elsewhere. |
| **F** | No meaningful brand. Unknown startup, effectively defunct, or dissolved. |

## Priority Tier Scale

| Tier | What it means |
|------|---------------|
| **0** | The hottest companies in the world. Frontier AI labs where the work defines the industry. A top PM would take a step down in comp or title to be here. |
| **1** | FAANG-tier. Large, stable, universally high-prestige. A top PM would choose this over a hot startup. |
| **2** | Highly respected brand, strong product culture, strong trajectory. A top PM actively pursues these when the right role appears. |
| **3** | Respected brand or strong growth trajectory — solid product culture. A top PM might take this but wouldn't lead with it on their next search. |
| **4** | Recognized brand, weaker product culture, flat or uncertain trajectory. A top PM takes this when options are limited, not as a first choice. |
| **5** | Early-stage startup with no meaningful brand but a credible funding signal — backed by top-tier VCs or notable angels, with meaningful capital raised. A top PM might consider this over a Tier 4 if the problem space is interesting and the investor pedigree suggests real conviction behind the company. |
| **6** | Strategic no-go. No brand, no AI, no redeeming value. Don't apply, don't engage. |
| **7** | Ethical no-go. Hard stop regardless of comp or role. |
